"My final-year dissertation examined how the EU AI Act interacts with GDPR, focusing on prohibited AI practices, risk classification, biometric surveillance, social scoring, manipulative AI and fundamental rights protections."

The research considered whether the Act gives organisations enough clarity to manage compliance across data protection, algorithmic accountability and cross-border AI deployment. The analysis maps the Act's risk-classification system against existing data protection obligations, identifying where the two regimes complement each other and where they produce conflicting compliance requirements.

Cross-border compliance is a recurring theme — particularly the UK's regulatory divergence post-Brexit, which creates a fragmented landscape for AI deployment across the Channel. The dissertation concludes with an assessment of the Act's adequacy as a framework for governing responsible AI in data-intensive environments.
